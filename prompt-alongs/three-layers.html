The Three Layers of AI-Native Work

A new mental model for knowledge work — and where you fit in it.

Knowledge work has always been a cottage industry. You went to school, learned some approaches, and then you developed your own idiosyncratic way of getting things done. Your methods lived in your head. Your value was inseparable from your personal process. That era is ending.

Not because you're being replaced — but because the structure of the work itself is changing. AI doesn't just help you do your existing job faster. It splits the job into layers, and each layer requires a different kind of attention.

You're not learning a tool. You're learning a new structure for work itself.

This tutorial walks you through that structure by applying it to a real task from your own job. By the end, you'll have decomposed one piece of your work into a system that AI can execute — and you'll see exactly where your human value concentrates.

The old model
You carry context in your head
You decide what to do
You do the work
You evaluate the output
Everything is you
→
The new model
Context lives in a data layer
You set the intent
AI handles the execution
You verify the outcomes
Layers have clear owners

In this prompt-along, you'll use Claude to decompose a task you actually do at work. You'll extract the data layer, write the execution layer, run it, and then verify the output. Along the way, you'll see where the gaps in your own knowledge live — and what that means for your career.

What you'll need
Access to Claude (claude.ai, Claude Cowork, or Claude Code — any of them work). A task you do regularly at work — something you could describe to a colleague who was covering for you. That's it.

Before we touch Claude, let's establish the mental model. All knowledge work — regardless of field — can be decomposed into three layers. This isn't a metaphor. It's a structural description of how AI-native organizations are reorganizing work right now.

Layer 3 — Top
Intent & Verification
What should exist? Why? And did the output actually achieve it? This is where you decide what to build, what problem to solve, what value to create — and where you evaluate whether the result served its purpose.
Human layer
Layer 2 — Middle
Execution
The how and the what of what gets done. The steps, the transformations, the logic, the production of output. This is the work itself — the part that used to take most of your time.
AI layer (human-managed)
Layer 1 — Foundation
Data & Context
Everything the system needs to know to do the work. Domain knowledge, examples, brand guidelines, past outputs, customer information, institutional knowledge. The substrate everything runs on.
AI layer (human-managed)
The key insight
AI handles the data layer and the execution layer. Humans sit at intent and verification. But — and this is critical — humans are responsible for all three layers. You don't do the execution, but you manage the instructions. You don't maintain the data manually, but you decide what context the system needs and you're accountable for its quality. Your levers for improving the system are: improve the data, improve the instructions, and sharpen your verification.

This is true whether you're in sales, UX, marketing, engineering, analytics, learning design, or operations. The three layers are universal. The specifics change. The structure doesn't.

Pause and think
Before you continue, pick a task you do regularly at work. It should be something substantial enough that it takes you real effort — not "send an email" but something like "write a weekly status report" or "qualify incoming leads" or "create a project plan from meeting notes" or "prepare a client presentation." You'll use this task for the rest of the tutorial.

You've picked your task. Now we're going to do something that feels simple but is actually the hardest skill in AI-native work: you're going to externalize everything you know about how you do this task. Right now, that knowledge lives in your head. We need to get it out.

The gap between what you know and what you can explain is the gap between you and an AI system.

Every piece of context you can't articulate is a piece of context AI won't have. And when AI fills in the gaps, it guesses — and its guesses are generic, not customer-centric.

Prompt #1 — Externalize your task knowledge
I want to decompose a task I do regularly at work into a system that AI can execute. The task is: [DESCRIBE YOUR TASK IN 1-2 SENTENCES]. Before we build anything, I need to externalize everything I know about how I do this task. Interview me. Ask me questions about: - What information do I need before I start this task? Where does it come from? - What does the actual process look like step by step? What decisions do I make along the way? - What does a great output look like vs. a mediocre one? How do I tell the difference? - What context or judgment do I bring that wouldn't be obvious to someone new? - How do I know when the task is actually done — and done well? Ask me 3-4 questions at a time. We'll go several rounds. Push me to be specific — if I give you a vague answer, ask me to give an example or explain what I mean. The goal is to get everything out of my head and into words.
This conversation is the work
It might feel like you're just chatting. You're not. You're doing the most valuable thing you can do in an AI-native workflow: converting tacit knowledge into explicit knowledge. Every detail you externalize here becomes data that makes the system better. Go 3-5 rounds. Don't rush.

Be honest with yourself during this conversation. When Claude asks you something and your instinct is "I just know" or "it depends" — that's the signal. Those are the moments where your implicit expertise lives. Push yourself to explain the "just know" and specify what "it depends" depends on.

You'll feel the gap
At some point during this conversation, you'll hit something you struggle to explain. That's normal — and it's the most important moment in this tutorial. That gap between what you know and what you can articulate is exactly the gap that determines AI output quality. Recognizing it is step one.

You've spent several rounds explaining your task. Claude now has a rich picture of what you do and how you think about it. Now we're going to extract the first layer: the data and context that your task depends on.

Prompt #2 — Extract the data layer
Based on everything I've told you, I want you to create a context document — a single file that captures all the data and context needed to do this task well. Organize it into sections: 1. **Domain context** — Background knowledge about my field, my organization, my customers, or my industry that someone would need to do this task well 2. **Input requirements** — What information needs to exist before the task can begin? Where does it come from? What format is it in? 3. **Quality standards** — What does good output look like? What are the criteria I use to judge quality? Include specific examples from what I told you. 4. **Edge cases and judgment calls** — The things that "depend" — the contextual decisions that require understanding the situation. Document the factors that influence these decisions. 5. **What's still in my head** — Be honest. Based on our conversation, what areas did I struggle to explain, or where did my answers stay vague? List what you think is still implicit and not yet documented. That last section is the most important one. Don't be polite about it — tell me where the gaps are.
Section 5 is the mirror
Most people skip this. Don't. The "what's still in my head" section tells you exactly where your system will produce mediocre output — because that's where the AI doesn't have enough context to make good decisions. Every gap here is a lever you can pull later to make the system better.
Comprehension check

Read the context document Claude produced. For each section, ask yourself:

  1. Is this accurate? Did Claude capture what I actually said, or did it add generic filler?
  2. Is this complete? Could someone who reads only this document understand the context well enough to do the task?
  3. What's missing? What did I forget to mention? What did I assume was obvious?

If you find gaps, tell Claude. Fill them in now. This document is the foundation — everything downstream depends on it.

This is the data layer. In an AI-native organization, this layer exists as persistent documentation — not as knowledge locked in someone's head. The quality of this layer directly determines the quality of everything built on top of it.

The data layer tells the system what it needs to know. The execution layer tells it what to do. This is where you write the instructions — not for a person, but for a system. The precision required here is different from anything you've written before.

Instructions for humans vs. instructions for AI.

When you brief a colleague, they fill in gaps with judgment, context, and a Slack message asking "did you mean X or Y?" AI doesn't have that layer. It builds what you describe. If the description is ambiguous, the output is a guess.

Prompt #3 — Write the execution instructions
Now I want you to write the execution instructions for this task. This is a step-by-step specification that tells an AI system exactly how to do the work, assuming it has access to the context document we just created. Write it as a clear process document with: 1. **Inputs** — What the system receives to start. Be specific about format and source. 2. **Steps** — The exact sequence of operations, in order. For each step, include: - What to do - What to reference (which part of the context document) - What decisions to make and how to make them - What the output of this step feeds into 3. **Output specification** — What the final output should look like. Format, length, structure, tone. Be precise enough that two different AI sessions would produce structurally similar results. 4. **Guardrails** — What the system should never do. Common mistakes to avoid. Boundaries on tone, content, or scope. Write this as if you're programming a very capable but very literal system. It will follow these instructions exactly. Anything left ambiguous will be interpreted by the system's best guess — not by human judgment.
The precision test
Read the execution instructions Claude drafted. Now imagine you're handing these to a brand new contractor who has never worked with you before. They can read the context document and these instructions — nothing else. No Slack, no meetings, no "quick question." Would the output be good? Where would it fall apart? Those failure points are your next editing targets.

If something feels underspecified, tell Claude and refine it together. This is iterative. The first draft is never precise enough. The third draft usually is.

You're learning the highest-leverage skill in AI-native work
The ability to write execution instructions that are precise enough for a system to follow — without human intervention — is what separates people who use AI effectively from people who spend their time correcting AI output. This skill transfers to every AI tool, every task, every domain. It's not about prompting. It's about thinking clearly enough to describe what you want with no ambiguity.

You have the data layer and the execution layer. Now we let the system run. You're going to hand Claude both documents and a real input, and watch it execute the task you used to do yourself.

Prompt #4 — Execute the task
Now I want you to actually do the task. Here's how: 1. Reference the context document we built (the data layer) for all background knowledge, quality standards, and edge case handling. 2. Follow the execution instructions we wrote (the execution layer) step by step. 3. Here is the input for this run: [PASTE YOUR ACTUAL INPUT — a real status update, a real set of meeting notes, a real lead, a real brief, whatever your task normally starts with] Do not ask me any clarifying questions. Work entirely from the context document and execution instructions. If something is ambiguous, make your best judgment call and flag it in a note at the end — tell me what was unclear and what you decided. This is important: I want to see where the system works and where it breaks. Don't try to be perfect — try to be faithful to the instructions.
Why "don't ask clarifying questions" matters
In a real AI-native workflow, the system doesn't get to ask you questions mid-execution. It has the data layer and the execution instructions — and that's it. By telling Claude not to ask, you're simulating the real operating condition. The notes at the end where Claude flags what was unclear are pure gold — they tell you exactly where your specifications need work.

This might feel uncomfortable. You're watching a system do your task, and you can't intervene. That discomfort is the identity shift happening in real time. You're moving from "person who does the work" to "person who designs and evaluates the system that does the work."

Don't edit the output yet
Your instinct will be to jump in and fix things. Resist it. We need to see the raw output — warts and all — so we can diagnose where the system needs improvement. Fixing the output fixes one instance. Fixing the system fixes every future instance.

This is where your value lives. Not in the execution — in the evaluation. You're now operating at the intent and verification layer, which is the layer that never gets automated because it requires understanding what the output is for.

Verification isn't "does it look right." It's three separate questions.

Output quality. Goal achievement. System diagnosis. Each one tells you something different — and each one points to a different lever.

Prompt #5 — Structured verification
I want to evaluate the output you just produced. Help me do a structured verification by walking through three lenses: **Lens 1 — Output quality** Compare the output against the quality standards in our context document. For each standard, rate whether the output meets it, partially meets it, or misses it. Be specific — quote the standard and point to the part of the output that does or doesn't satisfy it. **Lens 2 — Goal achievement** The intent behind this task is: [STATE THE REAL-WORLD GOAL — e.g., "help my manager understand project status so they can make resourcing decisions" or "convince a prospect that our product solves their specific problem"]. Based on that intent, would this output actually achieve the goal if I used it as-is? Where would it succeed? Where would it fall short? **Lens 3 — System diagnosis** For every gap or weakness you identified: is the root cause in the data layer (missing or insufficient context), the execution layer (unclear or incorrect instructions), or the verification layer (standards we haven't defined yet)? For each issue, tell me which layer to fix and what specifically to change.
Lens 3 is the whole point
Most people stop at "the output isn't great" and either fix it manually or blame the AI. Lens 3 forces you to trace every problem back to a specific layer. Missing context? That's the data layer — add it. Unclear instructions? That's the execution layer — refine it. No way to evaluate quality? That's the verification layer — define the standard. Every issue has a root cause, and every root cause points to a specific lever you can pull.

Read the diagnosis carefully. You're building the skill of seeing through the output to the system behind it. This is the transition from "doing the work" to "managing the system that does the work."

For some tasks, you can verify the output immediately — you can read it and know if it's good. But for many knowledge work tasks, the real verification happens later. Did the status report actually help the manager make the right decision? Did the lead qualification actually predict which deals closed? Did the project plan actually prevent the risks it identified?

This is the hardest part of verification. You need a system for tracking outcomes over time. At minimum: keep a log. Record what you sent, what you expected to happen, and — weeks or months later — what actually happened. That log becomes the highest-value data in your entire system, because it connects outputs to real-world impact.

You've run the system once. You've verified the output. You've diagnosed where the gaps are. Now let's make this concrete: what are your three levers, and what would you pull first?

Prompt #6 — Map your improvement levers
Based on everything we've done — the context document, the execution instructions, the output, and the verification — I want you to create an improvement map. Show me my three levers and what I'd change in each one to get meaningfully better output next time. **Data layer improvements** What context is missing or thin? What knowledge am I still carrying in my head that isn't in the document? What examples, templates, or reference material would make the biggest difference if I added them? **Execution layer improvements** Which instructions are too vague? Where did the system have to guess? Which steps need more specificity, and which are overspecified and could be simplified? What guardrails are missing? **Verification layer improvements** What quality standards don't exist yet but should? What outcomes should I be tracking over time? How would I set up a lightweight system to check whether outputs are actually achieving their goals? For each lever, rank the improvements from highest impact to lowest. I want to know what to fix first.
This is your roadmap
Print this out. Screenshot it. Save it. This improvement map is the single most actionable document you'll get from this tutorial. It tells you exactly how to make the system better — and each improvement compounds. Fix the data layer and every future execution improves. Fix the execution layer and every future output gets closer. Define verification standards and you'll know immediately when something is off.
Your three levers
Data
Add context, examples, domain knowledge. The richer the foundation, the better everything above it.
Execution
Refine instructions, remove ambiguity, add guardrails. Precision in, precision out.
Verification
Define standards, track outcomes, build feedback loops. Know whether it's working.

Let's zoom out. You just decomposed one task into the three-layer model. Now let's see what this means for your role — not abstractly, but concretely, based on what you just experienced.

Prompt #7 — Map the role shift
I want to understand what just happened to my role. Based on the task we decomposed, help me see the shift. Create a comparison with two columns: **Column 1: Where my time went before** List the specific activities I described during our conversation — the steps I personally performed, the decisions I made, the context I held in my head, the quality checks I did informally. **Column 2: Where my time goes now** Map each of those activities to the three-layer model. For each one, tell me: - Does this become part of the data layer (context I maintain)? - Does this become part of the execution layer (instructions I write and refine)? - Does this stay with me at the intent/verification layer? - Or does it disappear entirely because the system handles it? Then tell me: if I applied this model to every task I do, what would my job description actually say? Not my current title — what would I actually spend my time on?
The identity question
This is the moment that makes people uncomfortable. If the execution layer is handled by AI, and I was spending 60-70% of my time in the execution layer... what am I? The answer: you're the person who understands the problem deeply enough to design the system, and who can evaluate whether the system's output actually serves real humans. That's harder than execution. It's also more valuable.

Look at the role map Claude produced. Notice what moved out of your hands and what concentrated in your hands. The things that concentrated — intent setting, verification, data curation, instruction refinement — those are the skills that define your value going forward. Not because someone decided they should be, but because that's what's left when execution gets automated.

Most organizations aren't. The three-layer model describes where things are heading, not where most teams are today. But you don't need your organization to reorganize for this to be useful to you personally.

Start applying the model to your own work. Build context documents for your tasks. Write execution instructions. Track what works and what doesn't. You'll get faster, your output will get more consistent, and — critically — you'll develop the skills that matter most as the shift accelerates. When your organization does start moving in this direction, you'll be the person who already knows how to operate in the new model.

It feels that way because repetitive tasks are the easiest to decompose. But the model applies to creative and strategic work too — the layers just look different.

For a creative task like designing a campaign concept: the data layer is brand knowledge, audience research, competitive landscape, and past campaign performance. The execution layer is the creative brief and the process for generating concepts. Intent is the strategic goal. Verification is whether the concept resonates with the target audience. The structure is the same — the content is different.

The question isn't "is my work decomposable?" It's "which parts of my work require genuine human judgment and which parts are execution that I've been doing manually because no alternative existed?"
